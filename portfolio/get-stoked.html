Responsive Website Redesign

Get Stoked

Get Stoked is a swim program that emphasizes personalization, patience, love of water, and safety, and is owned and operated by the swim instructor, Billy. Our team redesigned his website to accurately reflect his level of experience and qualifications, as well as incorporate new features that increased efficiency on both the customer's end and Billy's end.

Project Overview

Challenge

Though the business goals of this project include 3 new features, we uncovered a larger issue our target audience had with the current website. Parents want a swim instructor that they can trust with their kids, and the current Get Stoked website doesn’t accurately portray Billy’s level of experience with kids and expertise in water safety.

Solution

Create a more robust front page for Get Stoked that includes Billy's teaching experience and qualifications, and build an account portal for customers so they can schedule lessons, make payments, and view lesson progress.

Team

I collaborated with Laura Podsiadlo and Armond Tsaturyan. We each contributed to the research, ideation, prototyping, and usability testing. As we reached the design phase, I spearheaded the mobile prototype, Laura brought to life the desktop prototype, and Armond lead the usability tests.

Research & Key Findings

With a solid understanding of our stakeholder's business goals, we began examining the current Get Stoked website for usability problems using established heuristic principles as well as contextual inquiries with users. We also conducted interviews with users in our target demographic to get a sense of what is most important to them when looking for a swim instructor for their kids.

Initial Research & Development

Business Goals

As a small business owner, Billy needs a responsive website that integrates payment, scheduling, and a customer account portal that is easy to use both on his end as well as the user's end.

Competitive / Comparative Analysis

We compared our client's website against 3 other top swim school websites, and put together a C&C analysis. In comparison to the others, Get Stoked was lacking in homepage content – specifically qualifications, testimonials, and links to social media pages, like Yelp.

Working With Users & Key Insights

Interview Results

In order to gain insight into what customers want to see on their ideal swim instructor's website, we conducted interviews with 6 different people within the target audience of Get Stoked - parents with at least one child within the 2-5 age range. One of the most important questions we asked during the interviews was What qualities do you look for in a swimming instructor? The top answers we received included experience, safety, responsible, and patient – all things that Billy's instruction completely embodies.

Usability Testing

However, when we then asked the same group of parents to test out Billy's current site, they did not feel that it provided enough information about Billy himself or his teaching methodology. We discovered that the information most desired by the user was absent from not only the homepage, but the entire website. Unsatisfied with the information presented, each user would eventually leave the website feeling too skeptical about the company to continue.

  • The 3 must-have sections that every parent brought up included: 
  • - Qualifications & Certifications
  • - Teaching Methodology
  • - Background & Experience

Analysis & Information Architecture

After synthesizing our research, we were able to identify the target user and develop a persona. Through affinity diagrams and card sorting, we reworked the information architecture of the website. We then created our persona's user flow, which satisfies both the user's needs and Billy's business goals.

Prioritizing User Needs

Affinity Diagram & Card Sorting

To help uncover patterns in our research, we created an affinity diagram to organize and group the key concepts from our interviews and C&C analysis. We took the resulting categories from the affinity diagram and used them as the topics in our card sort.

  • Users found the following 4 concepts the most important for their account portal:
  • - Child's Info
  • - Swim Calendar
  • - Progress
  • - Payment

Persona & User Flow

The recurring concepts we found in the affinity diagram also help shape our persona, Safety Sally. Keeping in mind both the needs of our persona and the goals of Get Stoked, we created a user flow that would satisfy both Safety Sally's need to fully understand the swim instructor's qualifications and teaching methodology, as well as Billy's goal to implement payment, scheduling, and customer accounts into his company's website.

Designing, Testing, & Iterating

Once we determined our user flow, we were able to create low fidelity sketches develop them into high fidelity prototypes. We tested and iterated our final mobile and web prototypes to make sure we resolved all of the user's frustrations.

Sketching & Testing

Paper Prototype & User Feedback

We started by sketching the mobile and desktop solutions simultaneously to ensure consistency. We then turned them into paper prototypes and conducted user tests to find pain points in the design.

  • The key insights we derived from our usability tests were:
  • - Qualifications should be made more prominent
  • - Testimonials are not very helpful because they are curated by the company, and therefore biased
  • - Users want to be able to view the instructor's availability first before signing up

High Fidelity Prototypes

Final Prototypes

The final prototypes were tested and iterated until all pain points were resolved. Some of the final changes we made based on user feedback included:

  • - Users want have the option to pay for their lesson immediately after booking it
  • - Users want to be able to choose their preferred method of contact in their account settings
Next Steps

For future iterations, we would like to undertake more user testing on the messaging feature for early check-in according to feedback we received. We would also like to perform Red Route analysis to find common user patterns for the rest of the app.
